
An In-Depth Analysis of the Parabolic Stop and Reverse (SAR) Indicator


Section I: Fundamental Concepts and Indicator Genesis

The Parabolic Stop and Reverse (SAR) is a technical analysis indicator developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. to identify potential reversals in the price direction of traded assets.1 It belongs to the category of trend-following indicators and is based on both price and time.3
The indicator was first introduced to the public in Wilder's 1978 book, New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems.4 This publication is considered a foundational text in modern technical analysis, as it also introduced several other seminal indicators, including the Relative Strength Index (RSI), the Average True Range (ATR), and the Directional Movement Concept (ADX).4
The name "Parabolic SAR" is not merely descriptive; it is a literal instruction that defines its core mechanical function. The "SAR" stands for "Stop and Reverse," which outlines a specific trading system.7 This system is designed to always be in the market, assuming a trader is fully invested in either a long or short position at any given time.7 When the criteria for one trend direction are violated, the system does not simply signal an "exit"; it signals a "Stop" of the current trade and an immediate "Reverse" into the opposite position.3 This design makes the Parabolic SAR, when used as intended, the basis for a complete, highly active, and purely mechanical trading system.2
Understanding the historical context of its release is critical. The Parabolic SAR was not designed to be used in isolation. It was presented as one component of a comprehensive trading toolkit. Wilder himself acknowledged the indicator's primary weakness—its unreliability in sideways, non-trending markets. To compensate for this, he explicitly recommended that traders first use the Parabolic SAR to establish the direction of the trend, and then use a different indicator from his toolkit, such as the Average Directional Index (ADX), to measure the strength of that trend.1 This intended synthesis implies that the indicator's well-known "whipsaws" (false signals) in choppy markets are a known design limitation meant to be filtered by its companion indicators.10

Section II: Visual Interpretation and Market Mechanics

On a price chart, the Parabolic SAR is rendered as a series of dots, or parabolas, plotted either above or below the price bars (e.g., candles).2 The interpretation of these dots is direct and binary:
Bullish Signal (Uptrend): When the dots are plotted below the current price candles, it signifies a bullish trend.7
Bearish Signal (Downtrend): When the dots are plotted above the current price candles, it signifies a bearish trend.7
Reversal Signal: A trend reversal is signaled when the dots "flip" from one side of the price to the other.3 A flip from below-to-above indicates a potential bearish reversal (an exit for longs and an entry for shorts). A flip from above-to-below indicates a potential bullish reversal (an exit for shorts and an entry for longs).7 Some more conservative traders may wait for three consecutive dots to form on the opposite side before confirming the reversal as valid.15
A more sophisticated analysis goes beyond this binary "flip" signal and examines the mechanics of the parabola itself. The distance between the SAR dot and the asset's price is a dynamic measure of trend momentum. The indicator is designed to "accelerate" its movement as the trend progresses.5 The rate of this acceleration is telling: a faster, more aggressive parabolic curve reflects strengthening momentum, whereas a "slowing" or flattening parabola indicates that momentum is fading.16 This allows a trader to visually identify potential trend exhaustion before the price actually crosses the dot, serving as an early warning to tighten stops or prepare for an exit.
Furthermore, the SAR dots function as a dynamic, parabolic "support" or "resistance" level.1
In an uptrend, the series of dots below the price acts as a moving support line.
In a downtrend, the series of dots above the price acts as a moving resistance line.
This reframes the indicator's utility. Instead of merely waiting for a crossover to exit a trade, a trader can observe how the price interacts with the SAR line. For example, during a strong uptrend, a minor price correction that dips to touch or nearly touch the SAR dot below—and then "bounces" off it—serves as a powerful confirmation of the trend's health.17 This "test-and-bounce" can be a signal to maintain or even add to a position, whereas a clean break of the dot signals the trend's termination.

Section III: Calculation Formula and Parameter Analysis

The calculation for the Parabolic SAR is iterative and depends on whether the indicator is rising (in an uptrend) or falling (in a downtrend).4

Core Formulas

Rising SAR (Uptrend):
$Current SAR = Prior SAR + Prior AF * (Prior EP – Prior SAR)$ 4
Falling SAR (Downtrend):
$Current SAR = Prior SAR – Prior AF * (Prior SAR – Prior EP)$ 4

Key Components

EP (Extreme Point): This is the highest high price (for an uptrend) or the lowest low price (for a downtrend) recorded since the current trend began.4
AF (Acceleration Factor): This is a multiplier that dictates the sensitivity and rate of acceleration of the SAR parabola.16 The AF starts at an initial value and increases by a set "step" each time a new EP (a new high or new low) is registered in the trend.4

Parameters and Customization

The indicator is governed by three parameters: the AF start, the AF step (or increment), and the AF maximum.
Default AF Start: $0.02$ 10
Default AF Step (Increment): $0.02$ 21
Default AF Maximum: $0.20$ 10
Under these default settings, the SAR begins moving slowly and "accelerates" by $0.02$ for each new high (in an uptrend), capping its acceleration rate at $0.20$.4

Calculation Constraints and Behavioral Function

The calculation includes critical constraints. In an uptrend, the calculated SAR value can never be set above the low of the previous two price bars.1 Conversely, in a downtrend, it can never be set below the high of the previous two price bars.4 This rule prevents the SAR dot from moving inside the recent price range, ensuring it functions as a true "stop" level.
Even more significant is an implicit rule of its mechanics: the SAR value is not allowed to decrease during an uptrend, and it is not allowed to increase during a downtrend.4 This is the mathematical enforcement of a one-way trailing stop-loss. This rule makes it impossible for a trader to "give the trade more room" by moving the stop-loss further away from the price. Wilder designed this as a "guard against the propensity to lower a stop-loss" 4, effectively building a core principle of trading discipline directly into the indicator's formula. This reveals its design as not just an analytical tool, but a behavioral tool to enforce a trader's self-control.
Customizing the AF parameters represents a direct, quantitative trade-off between sensitivity and reliability.25
Increasing the AF (e.g., to $0.03$): This makes the SAR "tighter" and more sensitive, moving it closer to the price.4 This results in more signals and is preferred by short-term traders or scalpers.27 The drawback is a high susceptibility to "whipsaws" or false signals from minor price noise.
Decreasing the AF (e.g., to $0.01$): This makes the SAR "looser" and less sensitive, placing it further from the price.14 This filters out more noise and is preferred by long-term trend-followers.27 The drawback is that it is slower to signal a true reversal, resulting in more profit being given back when the trend ends.
The "best" setting does not exist; optimal parameters are a function of the asset's volatility (e.g., high-volatility cryptocurrencies require different settings than low-volatility stocks 29) and the trader's chosen timeframe.27

Section IV: Core Trading Strategies and Application

The application of the Parabolic SAR follows from its visual interpretation and its "Stop and Reverse" design.

Entry Signals

Buy (Long) Signal: A buy signal is generated when the SAR dots flip from above the price to below the price.7
Sell (Short) Signal: A sell signal is generated when the SAR dots flip from below the price to above the price.7

Trailing Stop-Loss Management

While the SAR provides entry signals 5, its unique strength and primary utility lie in trade management.14 The indicator provides a dynamic, non-arbitrary, and psychologically disciplined method for trailing a stop-loss.4 This mechanism solves one of the most difficult challenges in trading: deciding when to exit a profitable trade.7
The process is mechanical:
In a long trade: The stop-loss is placed at the level of the SAR dot below the price. With each new price bar, a new, higher dot is printed, and the trader moves their stop-loss up to match this new level.2
In a short trade: The stop-loss is placed at the level of the SAR dot above the price. With each new price bar, a new, lower dot is printed, and the trader moves their stop-loss down accordingly.2
This process continues until the price finally moves against the trend and hits the trailing stop-loss, at which point the trade is closed, and the trend is considered reversed.31

Filtering the "Stop and Reverse" System

The naive application of the "Stop and Reverse" (SAR) system—where a trader takes every signal and is always in the market—is highly dangerous.7 The indicator is mechanical and will always generate signals, regardless of market conditions.2 In choppy markets, this leads to a series of consecutive losses.
A professional application of the indicator requires a filter to discern which signals to act upon.2 The most common filter is a long-term moving average (e.g., a 100-period or 200-period MA) to determine the primary or dominant trend.10
With this filter, the strategy is transformed from "Stop and Reverse" to a more robust "Exit and Re-enter" system:
If Price > 200-MA (Primary Uptrend): The trader is only in "buy mode." They will use the SAR "Buy" signal (flip below price) to enter a long trade. They then use the SAR dots to trail their stop-loss. When the SAR "Sell" signal (flip above price) occurs, they exit the trade, but they do not enter the corresponding short trade. They remain on the sidelines, waiting for the next "Buy" signal to re-enter in alignment with the primary trend.10
If Price < 200-MA (Primary Downtrend): The trader is only in "sell mode," taking only the "Sell" signals (flips above price) and ignoring all "Buy" signals as mere exits.2

Section V: Limitations, Weaknesses, and "Whipsaws"

The Parabolic SAR's greatest strength is also the source of its primary weakness. Its effectiveness is confined exclusively to trending markets.2
The indicator's performance is notoriously unreliable in flat, ranging, sideways, or choppy market conditions.1 In these non-trending environments, the indicator will "whipsaw" back and forth.1 A whipsaw is a rapid series of false signals, where the indicator flips to "buy" just as the price moves down, and then flips to "sell" just as the price moves up.30 This results in a sequence of small, compounding losses.10
This "whipsaw" flaw is not a bug; it is an unavoidable, mathematical consequence of the indicator's core design. The SAR is built on the assumption that the price is always in a trend (either up or down).7 It has no "neutral" or "sideways" mode. Because the Acceleration Factor (AF) mechanically moves the SAR dot closer to the price over time—even if the price is perfectly flat—the dot is guaranteed to eventually cross the price. When it does, it will generate a "reversal" signal, even though no new trend is beginning. This is the root cause of the indicator's failure in ranging markets.1
This weakness was well understood by its creator and by modern analysts.1 Experienced traders view a series of whipsaws (e.g., two or three in a row 36) not just as "bad luck," but as a meta-signal. The whipsaws are the indicator's way of signaling that the market state is ranging and is, therefore, unsuitable for a trend-following strategy. The correct response is not to keep trading, but to stop using the indicator 36 and wait for a breakout or for a clear trend to re-emerge.10 This underscores why a trend-strength filter, such as the ADX, is not considered optional but essential for its proper use.1

Section VI: Advanced Strategies and Filtering Techniques

Given the Parabolic SAR's critical failure in non-trending markets, its practical application almost always involves combining it with other indicators to filter false signals and confirm trends.2
The most powerful argument for this combined approach comes from a 2023 backtesting study mentioned in Wikipedia. This study, conducted on 12 years of Dow Jones 30 stocks, found that using the Parabolic SAR on a standard OHLC chart produced a mere 19% win rate. However, when combined with a Heikin Ashi chart—a technique that inherently smooths price action and filters noise—the win rate improved dramatically to 63%.1 This stark contrast provides definitive, statistical evidence that the SAR's value is only unlocked when it is paired with a noise-filtering mechanism.
Several filtering strategies have been developed, each asking a slightly different question to validate the SAR signal.
Strategy 1: Parabolic SAR + ADX (The "Wilder Combo")
Purpose: This is the classic, Wilder-recommended filter.1 It uses the ADX to measure trend strength.15
Question Asked: "Is there a trend strong enough to be worth trading?"
Rule: Only take any SAR signal (long or short) if the ADX line is above a defined threshold, typically 20 or 25.11 If the ADX is below this level, the market is defined as "ranging," and all SAR signals are ignored. A complete entry signal would require: (1) $ADX > 25$, (2) DMI confirmation ($+DI > -DI$ for a long trade), and then (3) the $SAR$ flip.39
Strategy 2: Parabolic SAR + Moving Average (Trend Filter)
Purpose: Uses a long-term MA (e.g., 50-EMA, 100-MA, or 200-MA) to determine the primary trend direction.10
Question Asked: "Which direction should I be trading?"
Rule: As detailed in Section IV, traders only take $SAR$ signals that align with the MA. If $Price > 50-EMA$, only "Buy" signals are taken. If $Price < 50-EMA$, only "Sell" signals are taken.10
Strategy 3: Parabolic SAR + MA Crossover (Confirmation)
Purpose: Uses a dual-MA crossover (e.g., 20-MA and 40-MA, or 8-EMA and 21-EMA) to confirm the $SAR$ flip.15
Question Asked: "Is the new trend confirmed by short-term momentum?"
Rule: A trade is entered only when both signals align. For a long trade, the $SAR$ must flip below the price, and the faster MA (e.g., 20-MA) must cross above the slower MA (e.g., 40-MA).15
Strategy 4: Parabolic SAR + RSI
Purpose: Uses the Relative Strength Index (RSI) to confirm momentum.29
Question Asked: "Does the new trend have sufficient momentum to proceed?"
Rule: A long trade is taken only if (1) $SAR$ flips below the price and (2) $RSI$ is above 50 (signaling bullish momentum). A short trade requires $SAR$ to flip above the price and $RSI$ to be below 50.38

Conclusion

The Parabolic SAR has remained a popular and enduring indicator since its introduction in 1978 for two primary reasons.4 First, it provides a clear, visually simple method for identifying trend direction.7 Second, and more importantly, it offers a robust, mechanical, and non-emotional system for trade management through its built-in trailing stop-loss mechanism.4 The indicator's design, which mathematically enforces trading discipline by preventing a stop-loss from being moved against the trend, is one of its most powerful and unique features.4
However, the analysis confirms that the Parabolic SAR is an incomplete system when used in isolation. Its design, which assumes a trend is always present, makes it mathematically guaranteed to produce a high rate of false signals, or "whipsaws," in the ranging or sideways markets that characterize a significant portion of all trading activity.1
Statistical evidence of its unprofitability on standard charts (19% win rate) confirms that a "simple" application of the indicator is flawed.1 Its value is unlocked only when it is synthesized into a multi-indicator system. Its own creator intended this, recommending its use alongside the ADX to filter for trend strength.1 Profitable application is therefore not found in its simple visual cues, but in its complex and thoughtful combination with other tools—such as the ADX, moving averages, or Heikin Ashi charts—that can accurately diagnose the market state (trend vs. range) before a signal is ever taken.
