4. Exit Process: Users can exit the plasma chain at any time by submitting a "exit transaction" to the root chain, retrieving their locked assets.

- Security: The root chain acts as the final arbiter, providing security assurances even if the plasma chain is compromised.

1. Stop-Loss Order:
   - A stop-loss order is an order to buy or sell a security when it reaches a certain price, known as the stop price.
   - The primary purpose of a stop-loss order is to limit an investor's loss on a position. It automatically triggers a market order when the stop price is reached.
   - This order type is commonly used to protect against further losses in case the market moves against the investor's position.

2. Stop-Limit Order:
   - A stop-limit order is a combination of a stop order and a limit order.
   - It is triggered when the security's price reaches the specified stop price, at which point the order becomes a limit order to buy or sell at the specified limit price or better.
   - This order type helps investors control the price at which their trade is executed, as it prevents the order from being filled at an undesirable price.

3. Trailing Stop Order:
   - A trailing stop order is a stop-loss order that adjusts the stop price as the market price of the security moves in the investor's favor.
   - The stop price is set at a fixed amount or percentage below the market price for a long position, or above the market price for a short position.
   - As the market price moves, the stop price moves with it, but in the opposite direction, effectively "trailing" the market price.
   - This order type helps investors lock in gains while also protecting against potential losses.

4. Iceberg Order:
   - An iceberg order is a large order that is divided into smaller, visible orders (the "tip of the iceberg") and hidden orders (the "submerged portion").
   - The visible portion of the order is executed first, and when that portion is filled, the next portion of the order is released.
   - This order type is used to prevent the market from being overwhelmed by a large order, which could lead to adverse price movements.

5. Conditional Order:
   - A conditional order is an order that is triggered by a specific event or condition, such as the price of a security reaching a certain level or the occurrence of a specific news event.
   - These orders can be used to automate trading strategies and execute trades based on predefined criteria.
   - Examples include one-cancels-other (OCO) orders, where two orders are placed, and the execution of one cancels the other, and one-triggers-other (OTO) orders, where one order triggers the placement of another order.

These are some of the most commonly used advanced order types in financial markets. Each order type has its own unique characteristics and use cases, allowing investors to manage risk, execute complex trading strategies, and take advantage of market opportunities.

   - For most stocks, the LULD price bands are set at 5% above and below the reference price during regular trading hours.
