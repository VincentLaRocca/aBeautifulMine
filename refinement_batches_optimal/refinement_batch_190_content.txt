On-Balance Volume (OBV):
- On-Balance Volume (OBV) is a technical analysis indicator that measures the cumulative volume of a stock or asset, taking into account whether the volume is flowing in or out of the security.
- The basic premise of OBV is that volume precedes price. It is based on the assumption that smart money (large institutional investors) uses volume to drive price.
- The OBV line is calculated by adding the day's volume when the price closes up, and subtracting the day's volume when the price closes down. The starting point is an arbitrary value, typically 0.
- OBV is used to identify buying and selling pressure, as well as to confirm price trends. A rising OBV line suggests that volume is flowing into the security, indicating bullish sentiment. A declining OBV line suggests that volume is flowing out of the security, indicating bearish sentiment.
- Sources: Investopedia, StockCharts.com, TradingView

Volume-Based Trend Identification:
- Volume-based trend identification is a technical analysis approach that uses trading volume to confirm and validate price trends.
- The underlying principle is that increasing volume during a price uptrend, and decreasing volume during a price downtrend, can help confirm the strength and validity of the trend.
- Key volume-based trend identification techniques include:
  1. Accumulation/Distribution Line (A/D Line): Measures the flow of money into and out of a security based on volume and price changes.
  2. On-Balance Volume (OBV): As mentioned above, tracks the cumulative volume flow to identify buying and selling pressure.
  3. Volume Oscillators: Measures the rate of change in volume, such as the Volume Momentum Oscillator (VMO) and the Volume Weighted Moving Average (VWMA).
  4. Volume Divergences: Identifies situations where volume is not confirming the price action, potentially signaling a trend change.
- Volume-based trend identification is often used in conjunction with other technical analysis tools to provide a more comprehensive assessment of market conditions and potential future price movements.
- Sources: Investopedia, StockCharts.com, TradingView

In summary, On-Balance Volume (OBV) is a volume-based indicator that tracks the cumulative flow of volume to identify buying and selling pressure, while volume-based trend identification uses various volume-based techniques to confirm and validate price trends. - The NVI tracks the cumulative difference between the current day's close and the previous day's close on days when volume is lower than the previous day.
- The theory is that decreases in volume indicate that "smart money" is leaving the market, which could signal a coming downtrend.
- Increases in the NVI line are interpreted as bullish, while decreases are interpreted as bearish.
- The NVI is calculated as:
    - NVI = Previous NVI + [(Close - Previous Close) / Previous Close] x 100, if Volume < Previous Volume
    - NVI = Previous NVI, if Volume >= Previous Volume
- Sources: Investopedia, StockCharts.com

Volume-based Trend Identification:
- Volume-based trend identification refers to the use of trading volume data to confirm the direction and strength of a price trend.
- The underlying premise is that increasing volume on price moves in the direction of the trend indicates that the trend has momentum and is more likely to continue.
- Conversely, decreasing volume on price moves in the direction of the trend suggests a weakening of the trend.
- Common volume-based trend identification techniques include:
    - On-Balance Volume (OBV): Tracks the cumulative volume flow to determine if volume is flowing into or out of a security.
    - Chaikin Money Flow (CMF): Measures the amount of money flow volume over a given period.
    - Accumulation/Distribution Line: Relates price and volume to determine if a stock is being accumulated or distributed.
- These indicators can be used in conjunction with price action to confirm the strength and direction of a trend.
- Sources: Investopedia, TradingView, StockCharts.com

In summary, the Negative Volume Index and volume-based trend identification techniques are both ways to use trading volume data to analyze and confirm market trends. Ease of Movement:

The formula for Ease of Movement is:
EMV = ((High - Low) / (High + Low)) * Volume

Where:
- High is the current period's high price
- Low is the current period's low price
- Volume is the current period's trading volume

The EMV indicator aims to identify the momentum of a security's price movement by comparing the price range to the trading volume. A positive EMV value indicates that the price is moving upward with ease, while a negative EMV value suggests that the price is moving downward with ease.

Interpretation:
- EMV above zero indicates that the price is moving upward with ease.
- EMV below zero indicates that the price is moving downward with ease.
- A rising EMV line suggests increasing upward momentum.
- A falling EMV line suggests increasing downward momentum.
- Divergences between the EMV and the price can be used to identify potential trend changes.

Chaikin Oscillator = EMA(Accumulation/Distribution Line, 3) - EMA(Accumulation/Distribution Line, 10)

The Chaikin Oscillator can be used to:
- Identify the momentum of the Accumulation/Distribution Line
- Detect potential trend changes and reversals
- Confirm the strength of a trend
- Generate buy and sell signals when the oscillator crosses above or below the zero line

- 26-period EMA is the 26-day Exponential Moving Average of the security's closing prices.

3. Subtract the 26-period EMA from the 12-period EMA to get the MACD line:
