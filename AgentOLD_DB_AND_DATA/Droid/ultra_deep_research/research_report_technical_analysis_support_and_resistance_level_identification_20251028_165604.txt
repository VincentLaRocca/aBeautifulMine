Support and resistance levels are key concepts in technical analysis, which is the study of past market data, such as price movements and trading volume, to identify trends and predict future market behavior.

Identifying Support and Resistance Levels:

1. Support Levels:
   - Support levels are price points where the market tends to find buying interest, causing the price to stop falling and potentially reverse direction.
   - They are often identified by locating previous lows or areas where the price has struggled to break through in the past.
   - When the price approaches a support level, it is more likely to bounce back up rather than break through.

2. Resistance Levels:
   - Resistance levels are price points where the market tends to find selling pressure, causing the price to stop rising and potentially reverse direction.
   - They are often identified by locating previous highs or areas where the price has struggled to break through in the past.
   - When the price approaches a resistance level, it is more likely to be pushed back down rather than break through.

Techniques for Identifying Support and Resistance Levels:

1. Horizontal Support and Resistance:
   - Look for horizontal lines connecting previous lows (support) or highs (resistance) on the price chart.
   - These horizontal levels often act as strong support or resistance points.

2. Trend Lines:
   - Draw trend lines by connecting at least two consecutive higher lows (uptrend) or lower highs (downtrend).
   - These trend lines can act as dynamic support or resistance levels as the price moves along them.

3. Fibonacci Retracements:
   - Fibonacci retracements are calculated based on the Fibonacci sequence and are used to identify potential support and resistance levels.
   - Common Fibonacci retracement levels include 23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, and 76.4%.

4. Moving Averages:
   - Moving averages, such as the 50-day or 200-day simple moving average, can act as dynamic support or resistance levels.
   - When the price is above a moving average, it can serve as support, and when it's below, it can act as resistance.

5. Chart Patterns:
   - Certain chart patterns, such as head and shoulders, double tops, and double bottoms, can help identify potential support and resistance levels.
   - These patterns often indicate areas where the market may find significant buying or selling pressure.

It's important to note that support and resistance levels are not absolute, and they can be broken or shifted over time as market conditions change. Combining multiple techniques and analyzing the overall context of the market can help traders and investors better identify and utilize support and resistance levels in their decision-making process.

Support levels are price points where a downtrend is expected to pause or reverse, as buyers are anticipated to enter the market and provide support. Resistance levels are price points where an uptrend is expected to pause or reverse, as sellers are anticipated to enter the market and provide resistance.

Some common support and resistance trading strategies include:

1. Breakout Trading:
   - Traders look for the price to break above resistance levels, signaling a potential continuation of the uptrend.
   - Conversely, traders look for the price to break below support levels, signaling a potential continuation of the downtrend.
   - Traders may place buy orders above resistance or sell orders below support, with stop-loss orders placed just below/above the breakout level.

2. Retracement Trading:
   - Traders look for the price to pull back (retrace) to a previous support or resistance level, expecting the level to now act as support or resistance.
   - Traders may place buy orders at the support level or sell orders at the resistance level, with stop-loss orders placed just below/above the retracement level.

3. Trend-line Trading:
   - Traders draw trend-lines connecting multiple highs (resistance) or lows (support) to identify the overall market trend.
   - Traders look for the price to bounce off the trend-line, signaling a potential continuation of the trend.
   - Traders may place buy orders near the support trend-line or sell orders near the resistance trend-line, with stop-loss orders placed just below/above the trend-line.

4. Consolidation Breakout:
   - Traders identify price consolidation patterns, such as triangles or rectangles, where the price is confined between support and resistance levels.
   - Traders look for the price to break out of the consolidation pattern, signaling a potential continuation of the trend.
   - Traders may place buy orders above the resistance level or sell orders below the support level, with stop-loss orders placed just below/above the breakout level.

It's important to note that support and resistance levels are not absolute, and their effectiveness can vary depending on market conditions, trading volume, and other factors. Price Channels:
- Price channels are formed by drawing parallel trend lines connecting a series of higher highs/lows or lower highs/lows.
- The upper channel line acts as dynamic resistance, the lower line as dynamic support.
- Prices bouncing between the channel lines suggest a ranging market.

Pivot Points:
- Pivot points are calculated using the previous day's high, low, and close prices.
- The pivot point itself, as well as support (S1, S2, S3) and resistance (R1, R2, R3) levels, can act as dynamic support/resistance.
- Prices respecting or breaking these pivot levels can provide trading signals.
