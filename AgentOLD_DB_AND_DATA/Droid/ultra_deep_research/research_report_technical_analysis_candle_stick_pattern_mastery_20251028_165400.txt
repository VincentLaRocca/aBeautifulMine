Candlestick Patterns for Day Trading:

   - It signals a potential trend reversal from bearish to bullish.
   - Source: https://www.investopedia.com/articles/technical/02/091802.asp

2. Hammer Pattern:
   - The hammer pattern has a small real body (the difference between the open and close) with a long lower shadow and a short or non-existent upper shadow.
   - It indicates a potential bullish reversal after a downtrend.
   - Source: https://www.thebalance.com/hammer-candlestick-pattern-1031051

3. Bullish Harami Pattern:
   - This pattern consists of a large red (or black) candlestick followed by a small green (or white) candlestick that is completely contained within the first candlestick's body.
   - It signals a potential bullish reversal.
   - Source: https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/harami.asp

4. Morning Star Pattern:
   - The morning star pattern has three candlesticks: a long red (or black) candlestick, followed by a small real body candlestick (gap down), and then a green (or white) candlestick.
   - It indicates a potential bullish reversal after a downtrend.
   - Source: https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/morningstar.asp

5. Bullish Marubozu Pattern:
   - The bullish marubozu pattern consists of a single green (or white) candlestick with no upper or lower shadow.
   - It signals strong buying pressure and a potential continuation of the uptrend.
   - Source: https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marubozu.asp

6. Piercing Line Pattern:
   - The piercing line pattern has two candlesticks: a long red (or black) candlestick followed by a long green (or white) candlestick that opens below the previous day's close but closes more than halfway up the previous day's body.
   - It indicates a potential bullish reversal after a downtrend.
   - Source: https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/piercingpattern.asp

These are some of the top candlestick patterns that day traders often use to identify potential buying or selling opportunities. Over time, the candlestick charting technique evolved and became more sophisticated. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the system was further developed and refined by Japanese traders, who identified various candlestick patterns and their associated market implications.
